# Pipeline Versioning

Explicit version history of the two core algorithms: curation (paper discovery and selection) and writing (content generation and verification). Updated whenever either algorithm changes.

## Current Live Version (TL;DR)

### Curation (v3.2)

Fetches up to 200 papers from ArXiv (last 7 days), enriches them concurrently via Semantic Scholar (citations, references) and Hacker News (social signal). Scores each paper on a five-factor weighted relevance axis (35% topic match, 20% keyword match, 15% institution, 15% citation velocity, 15% social signals) using a weekly anchor document (`data/anchors/*.yaml`) that defines hot topics, boost keywords, and declining topics (30% penalty). A second LLM pass (Sonnet) scores narrative potential on top candidates via a 4-criterion rubric: Surprise Factor (30%), Concreteness (25%), Practitioner Relevance (25%), Results Reportability (20%) — penalising vague results. The two scores combine 50/50 into a composite. Greedy selection picks 8 papers (min 3, max 10) with diversity caps (max 2 per institution, max 2 per topic), relaxing constraints if minimum not met. Runners-up (relevance >= 4.0) feed Quick Takes. A two-pass architecture exists (LLM only for top 20, heuristic for rest) but is not wired as the default path.

### Writing (v4.3) — current live

Opus generates each article from the paper's full text (preprocessed via `prepare_text_for_llm()` to strip references/appendix and truncate at sentence boundaries). The generation prompt enforces citation grounding — every factual claim must cite `[section X.Y]`, `[Abstract]`, `[Table N]`, or `[Figure N]`. Output is structured JSON: title, hook, signal block, structured abstract (What They Did / Key Result / Why It Matters), content body, section labels, and optional hero figure number.

**Structure** (6 parts with word budgets): Hook (1 sentence, ~20 words) — must state a capability or finding, never a method description. Signal Block (2-3 sentences, ~60 words) — practitioner filter: what capability is emerging, how mature, what decision it informs. The Problem (~150 words). What They Did (~250 words) — every technique must be followed within two sentences by a concrete example or analogy. The Results (~150 words) — at least one specific number with baseline; limitations dual-framed as technical gap and production timeline. Why It Matters (~120 words) — each implication traced back to something the paper demonstrated.

**Audience ceiling** enforced: reader knows APIs, basic ML, statistical terms; does not know transformer internals, attention mechanisms, or math notation. Concepts beyond the ceiling must be explained by analogy before the technical name.

Word hard cap: 1000 words. Banned words: revolutionary, groundbreaking, game-changing, breakthrough, incredible, amazing, novel, utilize, leverage, state-of-the-art, obviously, clearly, very, really, actually, basically.

A second LLM pass (Sonnet) verifies each claim against the cited passage, classifying issues by severity; any critical issue or 3+ major issues rejects the piece. A rule-based style checker enforces banned words, required structure, word count, and citation density per the Style Constitution. Quick Takes for runners-up use 2-sentence abstract extraction (no LLM). Output is delivered as Buttondown email draft (lead piece full, others preview + link) and a static brutalist web archive with TOC, hero figures, and Signal boxes.
